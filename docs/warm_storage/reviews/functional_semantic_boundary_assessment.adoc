== Executive Summary

The boundary recut is *plausible* but *over-justified by its narrative and
under-delivered by its mechanism*. It is also addressed at the wrong layer.

Three independent observations support this:

. The Semantic Model did contain material that, under the literal hypothesis,
  is problem-space (chapter 03 Problem Statement; chapter 10 Roundtrip
  Walkthroughs; the "relevant ideas" half of chapter 05). The boundary review
  named this correctly. *However*, the same Semantic Model also contained
  contract material that *cites* those narratives as evidence; the
  problem-space content was not absorbed by drift, it was hosted there because
  the Functional Model had nowhere else to put it. "Absorption" overstates a
  structural accommodation as semantic decay.

. The split's mechanism is navigational: chapter 03 and chapter 10 are now
  stubs that point to the Functional Model; preserved-context chapter 06 in
  the Functional Model is a verbatim copy of the original text. No
  classification has been altered. No vocabulary growth has been arrested.
  Persistence-boundary mechanism content (chapter 08) was deliberately not
  moved. The recut is *real* but its effect is read-path clarity for new
  readers, not contract hygiene.

. The strongest *consumer evidence* the project has -- Adoption-001
  (worldbuilding) and the Dynamic Projection analysis -- does *not* point to
  semantic ambiguity in the contract layer. It points to projection
  ergonomics, missing column-role classification on projected frames,
  configuration boilerplate, and reimport-review workflow. The single
  semantic-contract finding from adoption
  (`BUG-RUNTIME-META-PERSISTENCE-BOUNDARY-P4A`) was triaged into the
  Semantic Model under existing contract chapters, not into a problem-space
  recut. The recut is therefore solving a problem that the project's own
  field evidence does not yet rank as primary.

Net: the direction is defensible; the framing of "the Semantic Model began to
absorb problem-space material" is partly correct but overstated; the
counterfactual cost of *not* doing the split is small; and the persistent
architectural gap the project would benefit most from closing is in the
Technical Model, not in the Functional / Semantic boundary.

*Verdict: Yellow.* See <<verdict>>.

== Findings

=== F1. The "absorption" diagnosis is partly correct but the underlying mechanism is structural accommodation, not semantic drift

The boundary review correctly identifies that, pre-split, chapter 03 (Problem
Statement), chapter 10 (Roundtrip Walkthroughs), and the "relevant ideas"
halves of chapter 05 were not contract material under a literal reading of
the working hypothesis.

But the *cause* the German Problemraum-Konzept names -- semantic terminology
generating further terminology and pulling problem-space framing into the
contract chapters -- is not what actually happened. What happened was simpler
and more structural: the Functional Model was narrow (ch01 ~20 lines, ch02
~80 lines, ch03 ~80 lines, ch04 explicitly pre-cleanup with no semantic
review). The Semantic Model phase opened with a real need for problem-space
framing to motivate the new vocabulary, and the Functional Model had no
chapter ready to host that framing. Chapter 03 of the Semantic Model is
where the framing went, because it had to go *somewhere*.

The recut now creates `ch05_problem_space_overview` and
`ch06_preserved_problem_space_context` in the Functional Model. That fixes
the storage problem. It does not, on its own, address the recursive-growth
mechanism the German concept names: chapter 07 still lists ~17 candidate
sets; chapter 17 Open Questions still lists `AuditOnly` and `DiagnosticOnly`
as candidate further sets. Those are gated by the Adoption Strategy pilot
selection criteria (chapter 15), which existed before the split.

*Conclusion*: the *correct* diagnosis is "the Semantic Model lacked a paired
explanatory home, so explanatory content lived in contract chapters". The
*stated* diagnosis is "the Semantic Model was absorbing problem-space material
via terminology growth". The first is a storage problem; the second is a
discipline problem. The implemented recut addresses the first.

=== F2. The split's mechanism is read-path clarity, not contract hygiene

The recut, as implemented, did the following:

* Reclassified chapters in the Semantic Model entry page as Contract Core,
  Explanatory Context, Adoption / Planning, or Appendix.
* Replaced chapter 03 and chapter 10 of the Semantic Model with navigation
  stubs that point at the new Functional Model homes.
* Created `ch06_preserved_problem_space_context/` in the Functional Model
  that contains the *verbatim* original text of chapter 03 and chapter 10.
* Created `ch05_problem_space_overview/` in the Functional Model that
  absorbs the German Problemraum-Konzept's framing in English, with an
  explicit *Scope limit* note that "this chapter is not an ontology, taxonomy,
  semantic-set model, lifecycle classification, registry vocabulary, or
  rule-ready contract model".
* Marked chapter 05's "relevant ideas" / "project interpretation" split with
  an internal note. The blocks were not separated.
* Marked chapter 08's persistence-boundary subsection with a Technical Model
  navigation note. The mechanism content was *not* moved.

What did *not* change:

* The set vocabulary (chapter 07, ~17 sets) is unchanged.
* The contract-evidence pattern (citing concrete FK-helper friction examples
  from `FTR-FK-HELPERS-POLICY-DRIVEN-PRIMITIVES-P5` inside Ambiguity Policy,
  Lifecycle, Conflict And Precedence, Contract Matrices) is unchanged.
* The mechanism description for the persistence boundary is still hosted in
  the Semantic Model.
* The "relevant ideas" blocks of chapter 05 remain in the Semantic Model.

So the recut buys *cleaner navigation*, *clearer scope statements*, and
*preserved-context anchors* for future readers. It does not buy contract
hygiene, vocabulary discipline, or growth-rate reduction. If those were the
real value, they have not been delivered yet.

=== F3. Material is now arguably misplaced *into* the Functional Model

The most concrete misplacement in the new structure is not material that
escaped the Semantic Model, but material that arrived in the Functional
Model under the rubric of "use case concepts".

`docs/functional_model/ch04_use_case_concepts/` contains four documents.
Three of them
(`business_calculation_workbook_roundtrip.adoc`,
`localized_resource_workbook_roundtrip.adoc`,
`worldbuilding_continuity_workbook_roundtrip.adoc`) are detailed YAML pipeline
recipes for forward and reverse paths, naming concrete project step
identifiers such as `contract_compact_multiaxis`, `expand_compact_multiaxis`,
`contract_xref`, `expand_xref`, `add_lookup_helpers`, `split_by_discriminator`,
`apply_workbook_view_sheet_mappings`, `compose_workbook_views`,
`validate_references`, and so on. They cite closed FTR identifiers
(`FTR-XREF-CROSSTABLE`, `FTR-COMPACT-MULTIAXIS`,
`FTR-WORKBOOK-VIEW-ROUNDTRIP-RECOMPOSITION-P4A`, etc.) as coverage. Several
discuss "current contract", "first feature stage", precedence rules, metadata
shape questions, and acceptance directions.

Under the working hypothesis literally read, this is not problem-space
material. It is contract-and-mechanism material in narrative form. The
chapter `_chapter.adoc` even acknowledges this: it carries a status note
saying "No semantic review performed during the move."

This material is also not new. It was already inside the project; the recut
inherited it as Functional Model content because that is where the previous
move parked it. The current model boundary does not have a story for what
this is. It is not problem-space. It is not contract. It is not implementation
structure. It is *use-case design narrative*.

If the hypothesis is to be honored, this content is either:

* problem-space (it teaches *what* problem the project is set up to solve);
* contract (it teaches *what* the steps guarantee under the rules); or
* technical (it teaches *how* steps are composed in YAML).

Pre-cleanup, it is all three, with the second dominant. The current location
is therefore at odds with the hypothesis.

=== F4. The Technical Model is the layer with the largest structural gap, and the recut did not touch it

The Technical Model has chapters that are explicit `TBD` placeholders:

* `ch01_structure/01_layers.adoc` -- single line: "TBD."
* `ch01_structure/02_components.adoc` -- single line: "TBD."
* `ch02_contracts/01_contracts.adoc` -- single line: "TBD."
* `ch02_contracts/02_dependencies.adoc` -- single line: "TBD."

The substantive Technical Model content is concentrated in ch03 (ADRs), ch04
(concepts), ch05 (registries with substructure), ch06 (architectural layers),
ch07 (guardrails), and ch08 (architecture details). These are real and
detailed, but the *backbone* chapters that the recut's own logic depends on
(Structure, Contracts/Dependencies) are empty.

The recut explicitly noted that chapter 08's persistence-boundary mechanism
ought to live in the Technical Model but stayed in the Semantic Model
because "the Technical Model does not yet have a paired chapter to receive
it". The hypothesis the recut is meant to honor therefore *cannot* be honored
in its full form, because the third leg of the tripod is not built. The recut
shipped under that constraint, but the constraint itself is the more
load-bearing finding.

A more architecturally consequential follow-up than further problem-space
work would be: name the Technical Model paired chapter for persistence-
boundary mechanism, fill in `ch01_structure` and `ch02_contracts` so that
they describe the actual layer map, then route persistence-boundary mechanism
out of chapter 08. That work has not happened. It is also not what the
hypothesis itself is being defended on.

=== F5. The growth-dynamic concern is real but already braked, and the recut does not add to the brake

The German Problemraum-Konzept's recursion pattern

[source,text]
----
contradiction -> new semantic term -> new edge case -> further terms
----

is observable in chapter 07's ~17 candidate sets and chapter 17's invitation
to add more (`AuditOnly`, `DiagnosticOnly`). But the same Semantic Model
contains chapter 15 (Adoption Strategy), which already imposes:

* "Operational symptoms" as the trigger for a new adoption slice
  (not "we have not yet classified this family").
* Pilot selection criteria (recent review pain, bounded surface, runtime
  relevance, roundtrip exposure, existing artifact gravity).
* A "rule-ready, not rule engine" posture.
* A "no big bang" rule that adoption proceeds family by family.
* A constraint that semantic clarification and runtime refactoring are not
  separable (a slice that does only one is wrong).

These rules already brake recursive growth. The recut does not add to them;
it does not, for example, ratchet chapter 07 by demanding a stated functional
need before a new set is admitted. The "Scope limit" note on the new
`ch05_problem_space_overview` says new classification *belongs* in the
Semantic Model under the Adoption Strategy slice rules -- it does not add a
new gate.

So the recursive-growth claim, taken on its own, does *not* underwrite a
recut. The brake the project already had (chapter 15) is doing the work the
recut is rhetorically credited with.

=== F6. Consumer evidence supports a different priority than the recut

Adoption-001 (worldbuilding) is the project's strongest piece of consumer
evidence. Its executive summary is unusually direct:

> Most findings were not related to missing transformation capabilities or
> architectural defects. Instead, the majority of findings concerned:
> projection ergonomics, workbook usability, model growth, configuration
> complexity, user onboarding, safe review workflows for reimport.

The three concrete adoption artifacts that came out of it are:

* `BUG-ODS-REIMPORT-REPEATED-CELL-MEMORY-EXPLOSION` -- a parser carrier
  concern. Not a model-boundary issue.
* `FTR-CLI-INTERRUPT-ERROR-UX-P5` -- a CLI UX gap. Not a model-boundary
  issue.
* `BUG-RUNTIME-META-PERSISTENCE-BOUNDARY-P4A` -- a contract issue: runtime
  metadata leaking into persisted `_meta.yaml`. This is a Semantic Model
  contract issue and was placed into chapter 08's "Persistence boundary"
  subsection -- the same subsection the recut intentionally did *not* move.

The strategic follow-up cluster (Phase 3d) is *Consumer Ergonomics*. Its
heaviest hitters are:

* `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5`
* `FTR-DYNAMIC-VIEW-COLUMN-TARGETING-P5`
* `FTR-MODEL-DEFAULT-CONFIGURATION-P5`
* `FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5`

The dynamic-projection design analysis (`review_dynamic_projection_analysis.adoc`)
locates the missing concept as

> Column-role classification as first-class, queryable metadata on a
> projected frame (or on the workbook view bound to it).

That is a *modelling* gap and is plainly contract material -- but its absence
in the current Semantic Model is not what the recut addressed. The recut did
not introduce projected-frame column-role semantics, did not reclassify the
existing helper-column metadata as the prototypical case of this gap, and
did not surface "projection contract" as a Semantic Model contract surface.

Read against this evidence, the recut is parallel to the project's main
operational pressure, not aligned with it.

=== F7. The hypothesis's binary cut has a structural blind spot the recut sidesteps

The hypothesis is binary: problem-space vs. contract (plus a third leg for
technical structure). The boundary review itself flagged that this binary
forces chapter 15 (Adoption Strategy) into a hole -- it is neither
problem-space nor contract. The recut resolves this by carving an
"Adoption / Planning" *navigation grouping* inside the Semantic Model.

This is operationally fine but conceptually significant: the hypothesis is
ratified in prose ("Functional explains; Semantic contracts; Technical
implements") and weakened in structure (Semantic actually houses contracts
*and* adoption process). If the hypothesis is the basis for the recut, then
the recut's own structure is already exception-laden. That does not refute
the recut; it does mean the hypothesis as stated is not the whole truth.

A clearer statement of the project's actual model boundary -- if one is
needed -- would be: Functional explains; Semantic holds contracts *and*
the discipline of how new contracts are admitted; Technical holds
implementation structures. The boundary work would benefit more from
acknowledging that than from defending the binary.

== Evidence

The findings above rely on the following direct observations.

=== E1. Semantic Model chapter status, before vs. after

Pre-split, chapter 03 was a full problem-space narrative; chapter 10 was a
process flow; chapter 05 mixed inspirational background with project
interpretation.

Post-split:

* `03_problem_statement.adoc` is a stub that says the narrative was
  relocated under `FTR-FUNCTIONAL-SEMANTIC-MODEL-SPLIT-P5` and that incoming
  references resolve to the Functional Model.
* `10_roundtrip_walkthroughs.adoc` is similarly a stub.
* `05_external_inspirations.adoc` carries an internal-split *note* but the
  blocks remain inline.
* `08_lifecycle_and_update_semantics.adoc` carries a boundary-recut
  navigation note on the persistence-boundary subsection but the mechanism
  content remains inline ("Until that follow-up ships, the mechanism stays
  here in full, flagged as such, so that no content is silently dropped").
* `semantic_model.adoc` introduces the four-way navigation grouping
  (Contract Core, Explanatory Context, Adoption / Planning, Appendix).

This is the read-path-clarity result. It is real, but it is not contract
hygiene.

=== E2. Functional Model now hosts narrative spec material with explicit
"no semantic review" status

`docs/functional_model/ch04_use_case_concepts/_chapter.adoc` carries the
status:

> * Moved here from `docs/internal_guide/concepts/by_use_case/`.
> * Pending editorial cleanup under `FTR-CONCEPT-DOCS-CLEANUP-P5`.
> * No semantic review performed during the move.

The chapters themselves contain detailed pipeline YAML, named step
identifiers, missing-metadata policy notes, forward/reverse contract sketches,
acceptance directions, and explicit pointers to closed FTRs as coverage.
Under the hypothesis, this material is contract / mechanism narrative, not
problem space. The Functional Model entry path now hosts it as
problem-space material.

=== E3. Functional Model chapter 06 is verbatim-preserved Semantic Model material

`ch06_preserved_problem_space_context/01_problem_statement.adoc` and
`02_roundtrip_walkthroughs.adoc` are explicitly preserved-as-is texts whose
own notes acknowledge that "Contract obligations that this material
previously implied are owned by the relevant Semantic Model contract
chapters". The recut therefore created *two* problem-space chapters in the
Functional Model -- a new lightweight overview (ch05) and a verbatim preserved
context (ch06) -- where there had previously been one canonical home in the
Semantic Model.

This is justifiable as preserved context, but it is also a duplication of
problem-space framing: the project now has

* the German Problemraum-Konzept (cold storage; preserved verbatim);
* the English Functional Model ch05 overview ("absorbs framing" of the
  German);
* the English Functional Model ch06 preserved Semantic Model material;
* the Semantic Model ch01 management summary which still narrates the
  project's history and main thesis;
* the Semantic Model ch04 Ambiguity Policy, which still cites the same
  FK-helper friction examples to motivate its rules;
* the Semantic Model ch07 Overlapping Semantic Sets, which still uses
  problem-space prose to motivate the candidate sets;
* the Semantic Model ch08 Lifecycle, which still narrates "Why lifecycle
  matters".

Five to seven different surfaces now narrate the project's problem space
in overlapping ways. The recut consolidated *physical chapter homes* (03 and
10 became stubs) but the *narrative surface area* is larger after the recut
than before, not smaller.

=== E4. Persistence-boundary mechanism content is explicitly retained where the hypothesis says it should not live

The recut's own implementation note (FTR scope and `08_lifecycle_and_update_semantics.adoc`)
acknowledges that the persistence-boundary mechanism description belongs in
the Technical Model. The actual relocation was deferred because the Technical
Model "does not yet have a paired chapter to receive it". The semantic
boundary review (F4) had named this case as one of four load-bearing
decisions. Of the four, three were addressed (03 relocated; 10 relocated;
05 marked); one was deferred. That deferral is the highest-confidence
mechanism finding in the review and it is the one the recut did not resolve.

The result is that the recut's own most defensible call -- that mechanism
text is not Semantic Model material -- still sits in the Semantic Model.

=== E5. Adoption-001 surfaced ergonomics, not contract ambiguity

The worldbuilding adoption experience (`adoption_experience.adoc`) and the
adoption plan (`adoption_plan.adoc`) consistently point at projection-
management as the next product lever. Three of the four strategic FTRs
identified are projection / view / configuration capability work; one
(`FTR-PRESENTATION-DEFAULTS-SEMANTIC-CLASSIFICATION-P5`) is the only
semantic-classification companion and is described as "less directly tied to
the main consumer-side glue currently observed in adoption work".

The dynamic-projection design analysis (`review_dynamic_projection_analysis.adoc`)
names the missing modelling concept precisely:

> Column-role classification as first-class, queryable metadata on a
> projected frame (or on the workbook view bound to it).

This *is* a contract gap, but it is not the one the recut addressed, and the
recut did not bring it onto the Semantic Model's contract surface.

The single contract finding adoption did produce
(`BUG-RUNTIME-META-PERSISTENCE-BOUNDARY-P4A`) was handled by extending the
persistence-boundary subsection in chapter 08 -- the same subsection the
recut deferred.

=== E6. Recursive-vocabulary risk is bounded by existing project mechanisms

The Semantic Model already contains:

* Ambiguity Policy (chapter 04): distinguishes "must become explicit" from
  "may remain tolerated".
* Adoption Strategy (chapter 15): operational symptoms drive adoption slices;
  pilot selection criteria gate new families; "rule-ready, not rule engine".
* Open Questions (chapter 17): explicit container for unresolved contract
  questions, not implicit additions to chapter 07.

The recursive-growth pattern the Problemraum-Konzept warns about is therefore
already structurally braked. The recut does not strengthen the brake. If the
brake is the concern, the lever is chapter 15, not the model boundary.

== Counterarguments

A fair challenge must include the strongest arguments *for* the recut, since
the project's recent direction is being interrogated.

=== C1. Even read-path clarity has a real cost saved

The pre-split entry page led readers through Problem Statement and Roundtrip
Walkthroughs before reaching contract chapters. Reviewers and agents had to
reconstruct the contract-vs-explanatory distinction themselves. The four-way
navigation grouping plus the stubs make that distinction visible. For
project longevity and for AI-assisted review, that is genuine value. The
boundary review (F5) names exactly this kind of value: "keep the explanatory
pressure that drives new terminology in the Functional Model so that new
semantic vocabulary has to defend itself against a stated functional need".
The recut at least begins to enact that posture.

=== C2. The recut was deliberately small, and that was the right call

The FTR scope is explicit that this is *not* policy ratification of the
hypothesis. It states it is "the *first* operational application of the
hypothesis; subsequent reviews may sharpen or revise it". A reversible,
read-path-only change is a reasonable way to test the hypothesis before any
larger move. Under that framing, the implementation matches the design
intent and the small effect on contract content is by design.

=== C3. Preserved-context duplication is the right transitional state

Creating Functional Model ch06 as a verbatim preserved copy of Semantic
Model chapters 03 and 10 looks like duplication, but it preserves anchors
so incoming references continue to resolve. That is a deliberate hedge
against link breakage during the transition and is correctly reversible.

=== C4. The German concept's "absorption" framing is not load-bearing for the recut

A separate reading of the German Problemraum-Konzept is that it asked for a
*navigational* recut, not a *structural* one. Its chapter 06 explicitly
proposes the recut as documentation regrouping with the explicit non-goals
"no runtime behavior changes, no registry / runtime / implementation
changes". The recut delivered exactly that. The "absorption" rhetoric is
the German concept's *justification* for the recut, not a load-bearing
prediction about future behavior. Under that reading, the recut does not
need to prevent vocabulary growth; it only needs to clarify navigation.

=== C5. The persistence-boundary deferral is honest

The recut's failure to move the persistence-boundary mechanism is presented
as a deliberate deferral pending a Technical Model paired chapter. Refusing
to fragment a single coherent design description before the receiving home
exists is engineering discipline, not avoidance.

These counterarguments are real and partially neutralize findings F1, F2,
and the harshest reading of F4. They do not, however, neutralize:

* F3 (Functional Model ch04 contains contract-and-mechanism narrative);
* F6 (consumer evidence points elsewhere);
* F7 (the hypothesis is exception-laden in its own implementation).

| Consumer pressure is currently on projection management, column-role
  semantics, and configuration ergonomics. If model-boundary effort
  continues at the current pace, it competes with that pressure for
  attention and risks producing documentation churn without releasing
  the bottleneck the consumers actually hit.
| The adoption plan itself names this trade-off: "the framework's
  center of gravity is shifting from file transformation toward
  projection management". The boundary recut sits orthogonally to that
  shift.

| AR5
| The narrative surface area for "the project's problem space" expanded
  rather than shrank (E3). Five to seven overlapping surfaces now
  describe the problem in slightly different terms. The next
  contradiction someone notices between two of those surfaces will look
  like fresh semantic drift but will actually be recut-induced
  duplication.
| Either consolidate the problem-space surfaces (German -> English
  framing -> preserved context -> ch01 management summary narrative) or
  accept that drift between them is normal and not symptomatic.

| AR6
| The recut leans heavily on chapter 15 (Adoption Strategy) as the
  brake on recursive growth (F5). Chapter 15 is presented in the new
  navigation as "Adoption / Planning" -- a third zone. The hypothesis
  is binary. The implementation acknowledges a third zone. Future
  pressure to add a fourth or fifth zone (e.g., adoption metrics,
  registry lifecycle) will recreate the same problem at the
  classification layer.
| If the navigation grouping admits non-binary classifications, this
  should be stated as the actual model rather than as a temporary
  exception to a binary hypothesis. Otherwise the boundary will move
  again the next time pressure surfaces.
|===

== Question-by-question answers

The assessment was asked six questions explicitly. Direct answers, with
references to the findings above.

=== Q1. Is there a real problem?

*Partially.* The Semantic Model did contain material that, under a literal
reading of the working hypothesis, belonged in the Functional Model (chapter
03; chapter 10; the "relevant ideas" blocks of chapter 05). That part of the
diagnosis is correct.

The framing that the Semantic Model was "beginning to absorb" problem-space
material implies an active drift mechanism. The available evidence (F1) is
more consistent with structural accommodation: the Functional Model was
narrow, so problem-space framing went into the Semantic Model because there
was no other home for it. That is a different kind of "real problem" than
the one the project's own narrative claims. It is fixable by giving the
Functional Model a home for problem-space framing, which is what the recut
did.

The "wrong problem identified" reading is not zero. The recursive-vocabulary
growth concern (the German concept's named mechanism) is real but already
braked by the Adoption Strategy chapter; the recut does not strengthen that
brake. Calling the brake the "problem" and the relocation the "solution"
mismatches mechanism with intervention.

=== Q2. Did the split improve the architecture?

*Marginally and locally.* The split improved read-path clarity, made the
contract-vs-explanatory distinction visible in navigation, and preserved
incoming references. It did not change classification, did not arrest
vocabulary growth, did not move mechanism content out of the Semantic Model,
and did not address Functional Model ch04's contract-leaning content.

It also produced a side effect: the narrative surface area for the project's
problem space expanded across the German source, two new Functional Model
chapters, and the still-narrative Semantic Model chapters that cite the
same examples. The split did not consolidate; it added (E3, AR5).

Net architectural improvement: small. Net documentation reorganization:
real. The hypothesis is therefore best supported by the new navigation, not
by the architecture.

=== Q3. What still appears misplaced?

* *Functional-looking, located elsewhere*: management summary "Why this
  document exists" and "Main thesis" sections of Semantic Model chapter 01
  remain problem-space framing in the Semantic Model; chapter 04 (Ambiguity
  Policy) and chapter 08 (Lifecycle) still use FK-helper review-friction
  narrative to motivate contract rules.
* *Semantic-looking, located elsewhere*: Functional Model `ch04_use_case_concepts`
  carries contract-and-mechanism material in narrative form (F3).
* *Technical-looking, located elsewhere*: Semantic Model chapter 08's
  persistence-boundary subsection is mechanism description and is
  acknowledged as such, but stays in the Semantic Model because the
  Technical Model has no receiving chapter (F4, E4).
* *Empty homes*: Technical Model `ch01_structure` and `ch02_contracts` are
  TBD; this is the largest unfilled placeholder in the three-model layout
  (F4).

The pattern is consistent: the recut moved the two most obvious cases (03
and 10) and left the structural reasons (empty Technical Model chapters,
narrative-form contract material in Functional Model ch04) untouched.

=== Q4. Growth dynamics

The growth concern is *real* but is *already braked*. The brake is the
Adoption Strategy chapter, which existed before the recut. The recut does
not add to that brake; the new `ch05_problem_space_overview` Scope-limit
note routes future classification "into the Semantic Model behind the
Adoption Strategy's slice rules", which is exactly where the brake already
sat.

Best classification of the concern:

* Real architectural risk? *No, not yet.* Only one family (FK helpers) has
  been wired through; the candidate sets are descriptive, not enforced.
* Documentation issue? *Partly.* The hypothesis-as-stated does not name the
  brake; the brake is in chapter 15, which is classified as "Adoption /
  Planning" rather than as a contract gate.
* Review-process issue? *Mostly.* The mechanism the German concept warns
  about is "contradiction -> new term -> new edge case -> further terms"; the
  defense is review discipline (only stable contracts admitted; new families
  trigger through operational symptoms). That is review-process work, not
  model-boundary work.
* False concern? *No, but overstated.* The "recursive vocabulary growth"
  framing maps to real artifacts (chapter 07's 17 candidate sets), but the
  growth is currently within the project's own stated guardrails.

=== Q5. Consumer evidence

Adoption-001 (worldbuilding) and the Dynamic Projection analysis revealed
*ergonomics and modelling-of-projection* concerns, not contract ambiguity
in the existing Semantic Model.

* *Contract problems*: one direct case --
  `BUG-RUNTIME-META-PERSISTENCE-BOUNDARY-P4A` -- which is handled by chapter
  08's persistence-boundary subsection (the recut's deferred case).
* *Problem-space problems*: not really. The initial assumption "JSON <->
  Spreadsheet" was refined into "Canonical Model -> Editable Projections
  -> Roundtrip -> Canonical Model"; that refinement is *consistent* with
  the problem-space framing the recut absorbed into the Functional Model,
  but it predates the recut and did not motivate it.
* *Technical problems*: ODS parser memory explosion (carrier concern),
  CLI Ctrl+C UX. Neither is a model-boundary issue.
* *Documentation problems*: not the primary signal. The adoption report
  explicitly says the framework's core is mature and that the bottleneck
  is at the boundary between framework and users.

The Dynamic Projection analysis names the missing concept as
"column-role classification as first-class, queryable metadata on a
projected frame". This is a *contract* problem and is *not yet on the
Semantic Model's contract surface*. The recut did not put it there. If
consumer evidence is to drive the next Semantic Model move, this is where
the move should be aimed.

=== Q6. Counterfactual

If the recut had not happened, the project today would have:

* Chapter 03 and chapter 10 still in the Semantic Model as the same
  problem-space and process-flow content;
* No `ch05_problem_space_overview` or `ch06_preserved_problem_space_context`
  in the Functional Model;
* The persistence-boundary subsection still in chapter 08, exactly as it
  is post-recut;
* The same Adoption Strategy brake on vocabulary growth;
* The same set of consumer findings, none of which point at this boundary
  as the primary lever.

Would the project be *materially worse*?

* For new reader / agent orientation: yes, marginally. The four-way
  navigation grouping is genuinely useful.
* For contract integrity: no. The recut did not change classification or
  vocabulary.
* For consumer adoption: no, possibly slightly worse, because the project
  would have spent zero effort on documentation reorganization and could
  have spent that effort on Phase 3d (projection ergonomics) or on filling
  the Technical Model placeholders. Opportunity cost is small but
  non-zero.

The recut therefore traded *some* attention for *some* read-path clarity.
That is a reasonable trade, not a transformative one. The counterfactual
would not be a crisis; it would be a project with the same contracts, the
same vocabulary, the same consumer pressure, and a slightly bumpier
entry path into the Semantic Model.

[[verdict]]
== Verdict

*YELLOW.*

The split direction is plausible and the implementation is small,
reversible, and consistent with the stated FTR scope. The diagnosis is
*partly* correct: there *was* problem-space material in the Semantic Model.
The diagnosis is also *partly* overstated: the cause was structural
accommodation more than semantic drift, and the recut treats the symptom
(content location) rather than the named mechanism (vocabulary growth).

The recut delivers read-path clarity and preserves anchors but does not
deliver contract hygiene, vocabulary discipline, or growth-rate reduction.
It also leaves Functional Model ch04 (contract-leaning narrative), the
persistence-boundary mechanism (Semantic-Model-located by deferral), and
the Technical Model placeholders (`ch01_structure`, `ch02_contracts`)
untouched. These are the higher-leverage gaps under the hypothesis the
recut is meant to honor.

Consumer evidence (Adoption-001; Dynamic Projection analysis) does not
rank the boundary recut as the most pressing operational issue; it ranks
projection management, column-role contract, and configuration ergonomics.

The verdict is therefore *Yellow* rather than *Red* because:

* the implementation is genuinely useful for navigation;
* the deferrals are honest and reversible;
* no harm is done;
* and the framing of the recut as the first operational application of an
  unratified hypothesis matches what was actually shipped.

The verdict is *Yellow* rather than *Green* because:

* the named mechanism (recursive vocabulary growth) is not addressed by
  what was shipped;
* the consumer evidence points at a different lever;
* one third of the hypothesis (the Technical Model leg) is unbuilt; and
* the Functional Model now contains material (ch04) that, under the
  hypothesis, would itself be classified as misplaced.

This assessment does *not* recommend any rewrite, new FTR, backlog edit,
or further model relocation. It records the position that the project's
recent direction is *defensible but neither validated by consumer
evidence nor sufficient to discharge the diagnostic narrative it
inherited*.
