:toc:
:toclevels: 2

Review type: architecture / direction review.
Subject: `docs/technical_model/ch03_architecture_decisions/ADR-CONTRACT-FIRST-DOMAIN-META-REWRITE.adoc`.
Disposition tags: [Blocker-before-FTRs], [Slice-design], [Watch], [Non-finding].

NOTE: This review is preserved in cold storage as decision rationale for the
ADR. It is not part of the Technical Model's binding ADR surface.

== Verdict (answers to the three questions)

. *Do I agree with the assessment?* -- Yes. The disproportion between the
  user-facing transformation space and the domain/meta implementation is real,
  measurable, and already documented as concrete pain inside the repository. It
  is not a hypothesis.
. *Is it too radical?* -- No, not as a *direction*. The ADR is in fact the
  moderate option: it explicitly rejects the hard reset and keeps adapter
  preservation. The radicalism risk is not in the decision; it is in the word
  "rewrite" and in execution *scope* -- a direction this open can quietly become
  a bigger-bang than the ADR's own slicing intends.
. *Are you over-drawing the problem?* -- No. If anything the magnitude is
  *understated* (see measurements below: src and domain are larger than the
  figures quoted in the prompt). The honest risk is the opposite of
  over-drawing: under-bounding the *cure*.

Net: accept the direction; do not authorize rewrite FTRs until the
[Blocker-before-FTRs] items below are resolved, because they decide whether the
rewrite stays bounded or turns into a slow greenfield under a softer name.

== Evidence base (measured, not taken on faith)

All figures from the working tree at review time (`*.py`, excluding
`__pycache__`/`egg-info`):

|===
| Metric | Measured | Prompt claim

| `src` total LOC | ~22,432 | 17,351
| `domain/` subtree LOC | ~13,452 | 10,692
| `registries/meta_registry.yaml` | 919 lines | ~1000
| `registries/pipeline_step_registry.json` | 3,729 lines / ~116 KB | --
| Registered pipeline steps | 39 | --

|===

The quoted numbers are directionally right and conservative; the codebase has
grown since they were taken. The disproportion argument therefore *strengthens*
on verification rather than weakening.

The decisive signal is not the totals but the *spread of a single concept*. FK
/ foreign-key logic touches ~20 modules totalling ~6,029 LOC, including at least
four parallel homes for the same idea:

* `core/fk.py` (339)
* `domain/fk_relations.py` (348)
* `domain/validations/fk_helpers.py` (539) + `reference_validations.py` (775)
* `domain/transformations/fk_helpers/*` (633: enrich/drop/policy/provenance/formula)
* plus `helper_policies.py` (472), `derived_column_policy.py` (461),
  `transformations/helpers.py` (318), `column_roles/resolver.py` (296),
  `schema_maintenance/meta_update.py` (672).

And 8 of the 39 registered steps are FK/helper variants:
`add_fk_helpers`, `remove_fk_helpers`, `validate_fk_helpers`,
`reorder_fk_helpers`, `configure_fk_helpers`, `configure_lookup_helpers`,
`infer_fk_relations`, `add_lookup_helpers`. This is exactly the "one feature
accumulated lookup, enrichment, derived-column, cleanup, provenance paths"
pattern the inquiry describes -- now confirmed in code, not just asserted.

The demand side confirms the imbalance from the other direction:

* `personal_data` uses *4* steps (`validate_references`,
  `configure_workbook_view`, `add_validations`,
  `apply_workbook_view_sheet_mappings`).
* `project_memory` uses *3* (`contract_xref`, `expand_xref`, `plugin`).
* Only `worldbuilding` exercises the heavy families.

So 39 registered steps and ~13.5k LOC of domain stand against two of three real
consumers that each touch 3--4 steps. The complexity is driven by one consumer
(Worldbuilding) plus accumulated sediment -- which is precisely the ADR's thesis.

== Where the ADR is right and should be kept

* [Non-finding] *Timing argument is sound.* "No external adoption -> breaking
  changes are cheap" is true and the window is genuinely closing. Acting now is
  rational, not premature.
* [Non-finding] *Direction over reverse direction.* The
  intent->contract->implementation ordering, and the explicit rejection of
  "implementation sediment -> inferred model -> renamed sediment", is the correct
  antidote to the documented failure mode (ch06 problem statement: three
  artifacts -- code, FTR, docs -- each describing different semantics for the same
  `validate_fk_helpers` input).
* [Non-finding] *ADR hygiene.* Context / Decision / Alternatives / Consequences
  / Revisit criteria are all present and the alternatives are argued, not
  strawmanned. Good.
* [Non-finding] *"Direction only, FTRs still required"* is the right guardrail
  and should not be removed.

== Findings

=== F1 [Blocker-before-FTRs] The domain/meta boundary is named as a goal but never defined -- and meta lives *on* the adapter seam

*Observation.* The decision says "rebuild the domain/meta interior behind a
cleaner boundary" and lists "What should the new internal boundary be called?"
as an *open* question. The plan's central promise -- "preserve adapters, rewrite
meta/domain" -- depends entirely on that boundary existing.

*Evidence.* The meta lifecycle framework
(`ch05_problem_space_overview/03_meta_lifecycle_framework.adoc`) places the
Type-2 carrier transitions (sidecar <-> frame <-> workbook-embedded) *at the
render/parse boundary*: carrier-observed values (widths, orientation) are merged
back into meta on the read path; `_meta` is written into the workbook on the
write path. Meta and adapters are therefore coupled exactly where the ADR wants
to draw its preserve/rewrite line.

*Risk if ignored.* "Preserve adapters, rewrite meta" is not cleanly separable
until the seam is named. Without it you get the failure mode the reset strategy
itself warns about -- "old adapter assumptions leak into the new model" -- and
the rewrite stalls at the one boundary it most needs to be clean.

*Recommended action.* Before any FTR, answer the open boundary question
concretely: define the interface the adapters present to the new domain (what
shape of frames + meta crosses it, who owns carrier-precedence merging). Treat
the boundary definition as its own small design artifact, not an emergent
property of the first slice.

=== F2 [Blocker-before-FTRs] The ADR dropped two of the three elements the inquiry actually recommended

*Observation.* The inquiry's recommendation was a *combination*: contract-first
rewrite (strategy) + domain/meta reset *with adapter preservation* (boundary) +
*selective in-place simplification where deletion is obvious and low risk*. The
ADR keeps the first, folds in adapter preservation, but drops the explicit
"reset behind a boundary" framing and drops in-place simplification entirely.

*Risk if ignored.* A meaningful fraction of the 13.5k LOC is plausibly
*deletable in place* with no contract machinery at all -- e.g. the lookup-vs-FK
overlap that `personal_data` reports as unused, or duplicate FK homes (F-evidence
above). Routing cheap, obvious deletions through the expensive contract-first
pipeline wastes the rewrite budget and slows the visible win that keeps a
single-maintainer effort motivated.

*Recommended action.* Re-introduce in-place simplification as a *sanctioned
parallel track* in the Consequences section: obvious dead/duplicate-path
deletions proceed without waiting for a contract; contract-first effort is spent
only where conceptual debt actually lives. Reframe the title/decision from
"rewrite" toward "reset-behind-boundary + targeted deletion" so the scope reads
as bounded.

=== F3 [Slice-design] Contracts anchored on current consumer behavior risk golden-mastering today's workarounds

*Observation.* The First Contract Sequence is organized around the three
consumers. But the consumer checks are explicit that some current behavior is a
*gap to fix*, not behavior to preserve: Worldbuilding's empty-frame init, final
frame selection, and derived-column cleanup are all flagged as "should be
absorbed", and Project Memory's matrix context columns must stay display-only.

*Risk if ignored.* The strategy doc's own con -- "poorly chosen contracts could
freeze accidental behavior" -- materializes. Capturing the present cements the
consumer plugins that exist *because* the domain forced workarounds.

*Recommended action.* Require each contract to (a) cite the Functional-Model
use-case section it encodes, and (b) carry an explicit "NOT preserved" list of
current behaviors it intentionally drops. Contracts encode FM intent; consumers
are the coverage check, not the spec.

=== F4 [Slice-design] No per-slice convergence / kill criterion

*Observation.* The ADR has Revisit Criteria for the *whole decision* but no
tripwire for an *individual slice* that fails to converge. Contract-first
front-loads contract design; against 13.5k LOC and 39 steps that is a long
effort for one maintainer.

*Risk if ignored.* A slice whose contract cannot be expressed without dragging
in most of the old shape (one of the revisit criteria) is discovered late, after
sunk effort, instead of being a cheap stop.

*Recommended action.* Add a per-slice gate: if a slice's contract cannot be
written without importing the bulk of the old implementation shape, stop and
re-scope that slice rather than escalating to revisit the entire ADR. Keep the
first slice (FK-helper roundtrip) deliberately small -- it is the right probe
precisely because it is the densest overlap.

=== F5 [Slice-design] The lifecycle-attribute model may be the *cure*, not sediment -- protect it explicitly

*Observation.* Part of the meta weight (919-line registry, `meta_update.py` 672)
is *aspirational machinery*: ch05 states the target is attribute-driven Type-2
events replacing today's field-level branching, and that current code still
contains explicit field-level knowledge ("Current State vs. Target").

*Risk if ignored.* A meta "rewrite" that treats the registry as sediment could
discard the very attribute model intended to *remove* per-field branching --
throwing away the simplification vehicle.

*Recommended action.* Enter the rewrite treating the lifecycle-attribute model
(`drop_at_persistence`, `drop_if_stale`, `carrier_takes_precedence`, `required`,
`render_consumed`) as a *keeper hypothesis* to validate against contracts, not as
debt to be cleared. If contracts confirm it, it becomes the new meta core; if
they don't, retire it deliberately, with a reason.

=== F6 [Watch] The test suite (1.45x src) is unaddressed and double-edged

*Observation.* 192 test files / ~32.8k LOC. The ADR protects *fixtures* (right)
but says nothing about the unit suite, much of which asserts on the old domain
shape.

*Risk.* Either you keep tests that pin old behavior (anchoring sediment, the
exact thing you're fighting) or you delete large swaths during the riskiest
window (losing coverage when you most need it).

*Recommended action.* State a test-layer policy in the first FTR: which layers
are contract-level (keep/port across the boundary) vs implementation-level
(expected to churn or be deleted with the path they pin).

=== F7 [Watch] Single-maintainer + parallel-agent workflow amplifies the coexistence risk

*Observation.* The operating model runs multiple CLI agents in parallel. A
rewrite with old and new domain paths coexisting is precisely where two locally
consistent edits diverge -- the ch06 "three artifacts disagree" failure mode,
now with a moving target.

*Risk.* Transitional drift between old and new paths, harder to detect than in a
stable codebase.

*Recommended action.* Keep the coexistence window short *per slice*; honor the
ADR's "delete old path once the contract passes" immediately rather than in a
later cleanup; avoid running two slices that share the same boundary in parallel.

== Minor / process notes

* The ADR carries `Status: accepted` (dated today) while a second opinion was
  still being solicited. For a solo maintainer this is acceptable, but
  `proposed` until this review is dispositioned would read more honestly.
* "Adapter read-path safety" (repeated-carrier / `ParserLimits`) from the review
  instructions is a standing constraint on any adapter the rewrite touches; the
  ADR says adapters are preserved, so this should remain in force for any seam
  work that does reach into a read path.

== Recommended ADR amendments (concise)

. Define the domain/meta boundary before FTRs (F1).
. Restore in-place simplification as a sanctioned parallel track; reframe
  "rewrite" as "reset-behind-boundary + targeted deletion" (F2).
. Require contracts to cite FM intent and list intentionally-dropped behaviors
  (F3).
. Add a per-slice convergence/kill gate (F4).
. Name the lifecycle-attribute model as a keeper hypothesis, not sediment (F5).
. Add a test-layer policy and a short-coexistence rule to the first FTR (F6, F7).

== Bottom line

The assessment is correct and, on the numbers, conservative. Contract-first is
the right discipline and the timing is genuinely favorable. The plan is not too
radical as a direction; the real exposure is an undefined boundary (F1) and a
scope framing that omits cheap in-place deletion (F2). Resolve those two before
writing FTRs and the rewrite stays the moderate, bounded effort the ADR intends
rather than a slow greenfield under a gentler name.

Both first-pass blockers (F1, F2) are resolved substantively, not cosmetically.
The bridge-DSL framing is a stronger synthesis than F1 asked for: F1 requested
only that the boundary be *named*; the revision gives it a concrete contract
schema and ties it to a kill criterion (new Revisit bullet: revisit if the bridge
DSL cannot be derived from Functional-Model use cases without recreating most of
the current registry surface). The 919-line registry is now an explicit
reduction target ("a much smaller, lifecycle-aware contract than the current
registry surface").

=== Residual recommendation (R1) [Watch]

*Observation.* There is a slight tension between "the bridge DSL must be made
explicit before broad rewrite FTRs begin" (Consequences) and "the first slice
should be small to prove the bridge" + the per-slice convergence gate (First
Contract Sequence). Read literally, the first clause invites specifying the
*entire* bridge DSL up front.

*Risk.* Big-design-up-front on the bridge DSL could recreate the very
registry-scale surface the reset is meant to shrink -- the 919-line registry
reborn under a new name.

*Recommended action.* Clarify that "explicit" means explicit *for the slice in
hand*: the bridge DSL is grown incrementally, one slice at a time (FK-helper
slice specifies only its portion of the bridge), not authored as a complete DSL
before slice work starts. This is a one-line clarification, not a structural
change.

=== Overall disposition

All first-pass findings (F1--F7) closed. One watch-level residual (R1). The ADR
is, in this reviewer's assessment, FTR-ready once R1 is clarified. No blockers
remain.

Process note (carried, not blocking): the ADR still reads `Status: accepted`;
that is acceptable for a single maintainer, and the second pass does not change
it.
