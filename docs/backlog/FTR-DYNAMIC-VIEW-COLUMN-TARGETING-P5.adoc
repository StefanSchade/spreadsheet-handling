===== FTR-DYNAMIC-VIEW-COLUMN-TARGETING-P5 -- Declarative targeting of dynamic workbook view columns

*Status:* New

*Depends on:*::

* `FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5` (foundation; provides the
  column-role taxonomy this FTR consumes -- `row_identity`,
  `display_helper`, `matrix_value` -- and the reimport-survival rule
  for those roles).
* `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5` (the projected surfaces this FTR
  addresses) and `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc`
  (the recorded project decision for the projection mechanism). This
  FTR consumes the projected surfaces that decision produces; it does
  not re-decide the projection mechanism.

This FTR is a *consumer* of the foundation classification and of the
projection mechanism. It does not co-own either.

*Purpose:*::

Allow declarative rules to target dynamic workbook-view columns without
explicitly enumerating their concrete names.

The goal is not to introduce a second projection framework.

The goal is to let consumers address projected column sets *by
foundation role* -- e.g. all `matrix_value` columns of a projected
matrix surface -- for downstream concerns such as:

* validation,
* presentation,
* workbook constraints.

Addressing is *positive* (by role name) rather than by exclusion.
The three roles available are exactly the foundation taxonomy:
`row_identity`, `display_helper`, `matrix_value`. No richer class
system is presupposed here; if a use case requires one, it is a
separate future FTR.

Editability- and merge-back-related rendering are *not* part of this
FTR's motivating scope; they are excluded by the foundation FTR and
by the projection decision, and any future editability surface would
own its own targeting questions.

Note on terminology: where prior drafts of this FTR used "matrix
payload columns" or "visible payload columns", the corresponding
concept is now `matrix_value` per the foundation FTR. "Payload" in
the sense of `FTR-PAYLOAD-SEMANTICS-RECOVERY-P5` ("visible cells
whose meaning depends on metadata") is a *different* concept and is
not used here.

*Context:*::

Consumer validation in the `worldbuilding` repository exposed a
recurring pattern, here restated in terms of the foundation FTR's
role taxonomy:

1. A canonical relation is projected into a workbook-facing matrix
   view by the projection mechanism owned by
   `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5`.
2. The `matrix_value` set of that projected surface is dynamic and
   depends on domain data; `row_identity` (`story_id`) and
   `display_helper` (`title`) are known up front.
3. Post-projection concerns must then be applied to exactly the
   `matrix_value` set without enumerating concrete column names.

Examples:

* apply `from_legend` validation to every `matrix_value` column of
  the projected matrix surface,
* keep the rules stable while the dynamic axis grows with new
  entities.

Today this is possible, but not declaratively.

The validating consumer had to use a local plugin
(`add_from_legend_constraints_for_matrix` in
`worldbuilding/plugins/matrix_meta.py`) to inspect the projected
frame, derive the effective `matrix_value` list, and emit matching
metadata for each column.

This suggests a real product question:

Should the framework support declarative targeting of dynamic
workbook-view column sets *by foundation role* after projection has
already happened?

*Motivation:*::

This is strategically meaningful because many spreadsheet-driven maintenance
surfaces follow the same pattern:

* classification matrices,
* permission matrices,
* localization matrices,
* feature-toggle matrices,
* configuration matrices with helper columns.

In these scenarios, the important abstraction is not the literal
column name but the column's *foundation role* within the generated
view (`row_identity`, `display_helper`, `matrix_value`, as defined in
`FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5`).

If the framework cannot address such roles declaratively, consumers are
forced to reintroduce procedural glue precisely at the point where the
product should provide stability.

This is therefore not only a convenience issue.

It is a question of whether dynamic workbook views can become a first-class
maintenance surface in the product.

*Scope:*::

This FTR *consumes* the column-role classification owned by
`FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5` and addresses the column
sets produced by the projection mechanism decided in
`FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc`. It does not define
or redefine either. Within that constraint:

* Evaluate declarative ways to target dynamic workbook-view column
  sets *by foundation role* (`row_identity`, `display_helper`,
  `matrix_value`).
* Evaluate how role-based targeting should compose with existing
  validation, presentation, and workbook-constraint surfaces.
* Evaluate whether targeting should extend existing rule target
  shapes, live on a new dedicated targeting step, or be a shared
  helper consumed by multiple rule shapes.
* Evaluate applicability to validation, presentation, and workbook
  constraints.
* Record the intended long-term direction.

*Non-Goals:*::

* This FTR does not define or redefine the column-role taxonomy. The
  taxonomy (`row_identity`, `display_helper`, `matrix_value`), the
  reimport-survival rule, and the metadata-source expectations are
  owned by `FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5`.
* This FTR does not re-decide the projection mechanism. That decision
  is recorded in `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc` and
  is treated as input.
* No selector-grammar design, no wildcard / set-algebra surface, no
  composition operators. Addressing is by role name only; richer
  surfaces are out of scope and would belong to a separate later FTR.
* No editability or merge-back semantics, no collaboration semantics,
  no lifecycle modelling, no presentation-classification work. Those
  belong to other FTRs.
* No redesign of `contract_xref`, `expand_xref`, or `compact_multiaxis`.
* No replacement of existing explicit column targeting.
* No inference of business semantics from cell values.
* No mandatory removal of plugin extensibility.
* No implementation commitment in this FTR.

*Representative Example:*::

The consumer need, expressed in role terms (illustrative only -- this
FTR commits to no specific syntax):

[source,yaml]
----
- step: add_validations
  rules:
    - target:
        sheet: story_groups
        frame: story_group_matrix_view
        roles: [matrix_value]   # positive, foundation-role addressing
      rule:
        type: from_legend
        legend: story_group_codes
----

For contrast, the pre-foundation phrasing of the same intent was an
exclusion vocabulary:

[source,yaml]
----
- step: add_validations
  rules:
    - target:
        sheet: story_groups
        columns:
          selector:
            frame: story_group_matrix_view
            exclude_helper_columns: true   # pre-foundation; now matrix_value
            exclude_key_columns: true
      rule:
        type: from_legend
        legend: story_group_codes
----

The exclusion form re-derives by exclusion what the foundation FTR
names positively as `matrix_value`. The refined Option B uses the
role-positive form.

The exact syntax remains open. The relevant point is that the
consumer expresses intent against a *foundation role*, not against
concrete column names and not by exclusion.

*Architectural Options:*::

Option A -- Keep Plugins
+
Treat dynamic column targeting as a consumer-side concern.
+
Advantages:
+
* maximum flexibility,
* no additional addressing surface in the pipeline language.

-

Disadvantages:
+
* recurring procedural glue in consumers,
* poor reuse across common matrix-style use cases,
* weakens the product story for dynamic workbook maintenance views.

Option B -- Targeting by Role
+
Add a small targeting-by-role surface. Allow validation, presentation,
and workbook-constraint rules to name a foundation role
(e.g. `roles: [matrix_value]`) instead of enumerating column names.
No wildcard grammar, no set algebra, no composition operators are
introduced.
+
Advantages:
+
* reduces repeated consumer glue,
* aligns naturally with the projection FTR's role-based mechanism,
* keeps the surface minimal and orthogonal.

-

Disadvantages:
+
* introduces a small new declarative surface area,
* requires careful architectural placement (extension of existing
  rule target shapes vs new dedicated targeting step vs shared
  helper).

Option C -- Consume the Foundation Classification
+
Consume the column-role classification provided by
`FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5` directly. The *what* is no
longer speculative -- the foundation FTR names the roles and the
projection decision records how those roles reach the projected
surface at runtime. The remaining design question is *where the
consumption surface lives* (on existing validation / constraint
rule target shapes, on a new dedicated targeting step, or on both),
and whether any declarative ergonomics are needed beyond consuming
the role classification. This option is now the baseline against
which Options A and B are compared.

*Acceptance Criteria:*::

* The worldbuilding use case is evaluated explicitly, expressed in
  terms of the foundation FTR's role taxonomy.
* The evaluation explicitly states that targeting *reads* the same
  role classification consumed by `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5`
  and does not introduce a parallel taxonomy.
* Transferability to future business-oriented consumers is assessed.
* A project decision is recorded in a dedicated decision artifact
  analogous to `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc`.
* If declarative support is preferred, the design direction names
  only *role-based addressing* as the in-scope surface; any wildcard
  / set-algebra surface is recorded as a separate follow-up question.
* It is documented whether consumer plugins remain the preferred
  solution.

*Validation:*::

* Review the `worldbuilding` consumer implementation, focusing on
  `add_from_legend_constraints_for_matrix` in
  `worldbuilding/plugins/matrix_meta.py`.
* Review the `generic_loan_config` adoption notes for matrix-heavy
  business scenarios.
* Verify whether proposed targeting concepts would eliminate the
  current consumer-side metadata plugin.
* Verify consistency with workbook-view metadata and existing
  validation flows.
* Verify alignment with `FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5`:
  this FTR introduces no parallel role taxonomy and treats role
  semantics as consumed, not redefined.
* Verify alignment with the projection decision recorded in
  `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc`: this FTR addresses
  the projected surfaces that decision produces and does not
  re-decide the projection mechanism.

*Discussion Note:*::

This topic is adjacent to `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5` and to
`FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5` but identical to neither.
The three FTRs partition the projected-column problem along three
distinct axes:

* *How is the column-role classification defined?* -- answered by
  `FTR-PROJECTED-FRAME-COLUMN-SEMANTICS-P5` (the foundation).
* *How is a generated view shaped?* -- answered by
  `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5` and its recorded decision in
  `FTR-DYNAMIC-FRAME-PROJECTION-P5_decision.adoc`.
* *How do later concerns address the generated columns of that
  view?* -- the question this FTR is responsible for.

This FTR *reads* the classification (it does not define it) and
addresses the surfaces that the projection mechanism produces (it
does not re-decide the mechanism).

Editability-related rendering may eventually become a consumer of
role-based targeting too, but only once editability is itself owned
by another FTR. It is excluded from this FTR's motivating scope
because the foundation FTR and the projection decision both exclude
editability.
